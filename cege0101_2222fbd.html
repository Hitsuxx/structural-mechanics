Section 01
What Is a Free Body Diagram?
DEFINITION · PURPOSE · WHY IT MATTERS

A Free Body Diagram is a sketch of a body (or part of a body) that has been isolated from its surroundings, with every force acting on it explicitly shown as an arrow.

Key idea: Remove the object from its environment. Replace every contact point, support, and connection with the force or moment that point exerts on the body. Show ALL forces — including reactions and self-weight.
Why FBDs matter: Without an FBD you cannot write equilibrium equations correctly. Every reaction force, every joint analysis, every moment calculation requires knowing precisely what forces act on the body.

Section 02
The 3 Golden Rules
EVERY FBD MUST FOLLOW THESE
  1. 1
    ISOLATE THE BODY
    Draw the object completely free from all supports, connections, and surrounding structure. Use a clear boundary (a dashed cut-line or just white space) to show it is isolated. The body itself can be a whole beam, a single joint, a section cut, or even a small element.
  2. 2
    REPLACE EVERY SUPPORT WITH ITS REACTIONS
    Each support is removed and replaced with the forces (and moments) it applies to the body. A pin gives two force reactions. A roller gives one. A fixed support gives two forces and a moment. Do not leave any support in the diagram — the whole point is they are replaced.
  3. 3
    SHOW EVERY EXTERNAL FORCE AND MOMENT
    Draw all applied loads (point loads, UDL, moments), self-weight if relevant, and any forces from connections to other bodies. Each force has a magnitude, direction, and point of application. Label everything — unknown reactions get a letter (e.g. \(R_A\), \(H_A\)).
Critical: Internal forces (forces between two parts of the same body) do NOT appear on an FBD of the whole body. They only appear when you cut the body and draw FBDs of each piece separately.

Section 04
Forces & Load Types
WHAT GOES ON THE FBD
Load Type Explorer
A point load P acts at a single point. It has units of force (kN).
Point Load
Single force \(P\) applied at one point. Units: kN. Drawn as a single arrow at the point of application. Causes both shear and bending.
UDL (Uniform Distributed Load)
Load spread evenly over a length. Intensity \(w\) in kN/m. Resultant \(W = w \times L\) acts at the midpoint of the loaded length for moment calculations.
Varying / Triangular Load
Intensity varies linearly from 0 to \(w_{\max}\). Resultant \(W = \frac{1}{2}w_{\max}L\) acts at the centroid — \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the length from the zero end.
Applied Moment
A couple \(M\) applied at a point. Units: kNm. Drawn as a curved arrow. Contributes directly to moment equilibrium but not force equilibrium. Sign convention: clockwise positive (or state yours clearly).
Inclined Force
Must be resolved into horizontal and vertical components before writing equilibrium equations: \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(F_y = F\sin\theta\).
Self-Weight
Acts downward at the centroid of the body. Often neglected in structural analysis problems unless the problem explicitly says to include it (\(W = mg\) or given as a UDL).
Section 05
How to Draw an FBD — Step by Step
THE PROCEDURE EVERY TIME
1. Identify
2. Sketch
3. Supports
4. Loads
5. Label
6. Verify
STEP 1 — IDENTIFY THE BODY

Decide exactly what object you are drawing the FBD for. This could be:

  • The entire beam or frame (to find support reactions)
  • A single joint in a truss (Method of Joints)
  • A section cut through the structure (Method of Sections)
  • An individual member of a frame
The choice determines which forces are internal vs external. A force is external if it crosses the boundary of your chosen body.

STEP 2 — SKETCH THE BODY IN ISOLATION

Draw the body by itself — no supports, no ground, no other members. Keep the geometry accurate (to scale or at least proportional). Draw a light dashed boundary if it helps show what has been removed. The body should "float" in space on your diagram.

Tip: Draw the body shape first, then add forces. Never draw force arrows before the body is sketched.
STEP 3 — ADD SUPPORT REACTIONS

At every point where a support was, draw the reaction force(s) and/or moment that support provides. Use assumed positive directions — if the answer comes out negative, the reaction acts the other way.

Pin at A: Draw \(H_A\) pointing right and \(V_A\) pointing up (both assumed positive).
Roller at B: Draw \(V_B\) pointing up only (no horizontal, no moment).
Don't: Draw the triangular/pin symbol itself. The symbol represents the support in a structural diagram. In the FBD, you only draw the force arrows.
STEP 4 — ADD ALL EXTERNAL LOADS

Draw every applied load: point loads, UDLs (shown as a series of arrows with a line, or replaced by their resultant for moment calculation), applied moments, and self-weight if relevant.

  • UDL of \(w\) kN/m over length \(L\): draw the distributed arrows, note the resultant \(wL\) acts at \(L/2\)
  • Inclined force: draw the force at the given angle, resolve into components for the equilibrium equations
  • Applied moment: curved arrow at the point of application, labelled \(M\) with direction
STEP 5 — LABEL EVERYTHING

Every force arrow needs a label. Known forces get their numerical value. Unknown reactions get algebraic symbols. Dimensions and distances must be marked — you need them for moment calculations.

Reactions: Use systematic notation — \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\) for horizontal, vertical, moment at A.
Distances: Mark the x-position of every load and reaction. Moments need a lever arm.
Angles: If a load is inclined, mark \(\theta\) and the components \(F\cos\theta\), \(F\sin\theta\).
STEP 6 — VERIFY BEFORE SOLVING

Before writing a single equilibrium equation, check:

  • Does every support have its correct number of reactions? (Pin=2, Roller=1, Fixed=3)
  • Are all external loads shown, in the right direction, at the right position?
  • Is the body truly isolated — no support symbols remaining?
  • Count unknowns: for a simply-supported beam, you should have exactly 3 unknowns = 3 equations. ✓
  • Have you marked enough dimensions to write moment equations?
If the FBD is correct, the algebra will work. Most equilibrium errors come from wrong FBDs, not wrong algebra.

Section 06
Equilibrium Equations
WHAT THEY MEAN · HOW TO USE THEM · WORKED EXAMPLE

Static equilibrium means the body is not accelerating — it is either stationary or moving at constant velocity. Newton's second law says \(\sum \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}\), so if acceleration is zero, the net force must also be zero. In 2D this produces exactly three independent equations.

Equation 1 — No sideways motion
\[ \sum F_x = 0 \]
The horizontal components of every force on the body must cancel out. If they don't, the body would accelerate left or right. Positive = rightward (→) by convention.
Equation 2 — No vertical motion
\[ \sum F_y = 0 \]
The vertical components of every force must cancel out. If they don't, the body would accelerate up or down. Positive = upward (↑) by convention.
Equation 3 — No rotation
\[ \sum M_O = 0 \]
The net turning effect about any chosen point O must be zero. If it isn't, the body would spin. You can choose any O — choosing it cleverly eliminates unknowns.
Why exactly 3 equations? In 2D, a rigid body has 3 degrees of freedom — it can translate in x, translate in y, and rotate. One equation is needed to constrain each degree of freedom. This is why a simply-supported beam (with a pin and a roller giving exactly 3 unknowns) is perfectly solvable: 3 equations, 3 unknowns.
What happens when equilibrium is violated?
All three equations are satisfied. The beam is in static equilibrium.
What Is a Moment?

A moment (or torque) is the turning effect of a force about a point. The further the force is from the pivot, the bigger the turning effect — the same reason a long spanner makes a bolt easier to undo. Mathematically:

Moment of force \(F\) about point \(O\)
\[ M_O = F \times d_{\perp} \]
where \(d_{\perp}\) is the perpendicular distance from O to the line of action of F (the "lever arm")

Sign Convention

Before writing any equation you must decide what "positive" means. This is entirely your choice — but you must be consistent throughout the problem. The standard engineering convention is shown below. Notice that if you assume a direction and the answer comes out negative, that simply means the force acts in the opposite direction to what you assumed. That is not an error — it is the correct answer.

Sign convention — forces and moments
Good habit: At the top of every solution write your sign convention explicitly, e.g. "→ +ve, ↑ +ve, ACW +ve". This prevents errors when you have multiple moments with different directions.
Common error: Changing sign convention halfway through a problem. If you start with anticlockwise = positive for \(\sum M\), every moment in that equation must follow the same rule.
Choosing Where to Take Moments

The moment equation \(\sum M_O = 0\) is valid about any point O in the plane — the fixed end of a beam, a free end, the midspan, even a point off the beam entirely. You always get a valid equation. But the smart choice is to pick O at a point where unknown forces act — because a force applied exactly at O has zero lever arm, so it drops out of the equation automatically, leaving you with fewer unknowns to solve for simultaneously.

Worked Examples
5 FULLY SOLVED PROBLEMS WITH FBDs
EXAMPLE 1 · Simply Supported Beam
Pin at A, roller at B, UDL + point load. Find all reactions.
▼ expand

Problem: A simply-supported beam AB of length 8 m. Pin at A, roller at B. UDL \(w = 15\) kN/m over the full span, plus a point load \(P = 40\) kN at \(x = 3\) m from A. Find \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(V_B\).

STEP 1 — FBD

Isolate beam AB. Remove pin at A → reactions \(H_A\) (rightward +ve) and \(V_A\) (upward +ve). Remove roller at B → reaction \(V_B\) (upward +ve). Apply UDL (15 arrows over 8 m) and point load 40 kN↓ at x=3 m.

STEP 2 — EQUILIBRIUM
\(\sum F_x = 0\)
\[ H_A = 0 \quad \text{(no horizontal loads)} \]
\(\sum M_A = 0\) (anticlockwise +ve, find \(V_B\))
\[ V_B \times 8 = 40 \times 3 + 15 \times 8 \times 4 \]
\[ V_B \times 8 = 120 + 480 = 600 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{V_B = 75\text{ kN}\uparrow} \]
\(\sum F_y = 0\) (find V_A)
\[ V_A + V_B = 40 + 15 \times 8 = 160\text{ kN} \]
\[ \boxed{V_A = 160 - 75 = 85\text{ kN}\uparrow} \]
Answer: \(H_A = 0\), \(V_A = 85\text{ kN}\uparrow\), \(V_B = 75\text{ kN}\uparrow\). Check: \(85 + 75 = 160 = 40 + 120\) ✓
EXAMPLE 2 · Cantilever Beam
Fixed wall at A, free end at B with point load. Find all reactions.
▼ expand

Problem: A cantilever beam fixed at A, length \(L = 5\) m. Point load \(P = 30\) kN↓ at free end B, plus UDL \(w = 10\) kN/m. Find \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\).

FBD & SOLUTION

Fixed support at A → 3 unknowns: \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\). No other supports. Total vertical load = \(30 + 10 \times 5 = 80\) kN↓.

\(\sum F_x = 0\)
\[ H_A = 0 \]
\(\sum F_y = 0\)
\[ V_A = 30 + 10 \times 5 = \boxed{80\text{ kN}\uparrow} \]
\(\sum M_A = 0\)  ACW +ve — wall moment reaction
\[ M_A = 30 \times 5 + 10 \times 5 \times \frac{5}{2} = 150 + 125 = \boxed{275\text{ kNm (anticlockwise)}} \]
Note: The wall moment \(M_A\) opposes the hogging caused by the loads. It is a reaction moment — without it, the beam would rotate. This is the fixed support's third unknown.
EXAMPLE 3 · Inclined Load
Simply-supported beam with an angled point load. Resolve and find reactions.
▼ expand

Problem: SS beam, \(L = 6\) m. Pin at A (left), roller at B (right). An inclined force of 60 kN at 30° to the horizontal acts downward-right at x = 4 m from A. Find all reactions.

RESOLVE INCLINED FORCE FIRST
\[ F_x = 60\cos30° = 60 \times 0.866 = 51.96\text{ kN (rightward)} \]\[ F_y = 60\sin30° = 60 \times 0.5 = 30\text{ kN (downward)} \]
FBD & EQUILIBRIUM
\(\sum F_x = 0\)
\[ H_A + 51.96 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{H_A = -51.96\text{ kN (i.e. leftward)}} \]
\(\sum M_A = 0\)
\[ V_B \times 6 = 30 \times 4 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{V_B = 20\text{ kN}\uparrow} \]
\(\sum F_y = 0\)
\[ V_A + V_B = 30 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \boxed{V_A = 10\text{ kN}\uparrow} \]
Key: \(H_A\) came out negative — the pin at A actually pushes leftward, opposing the horizontal component of the inclined load. The assumed direction was wrong, but that is fine: the algebra corrects it automatically.
EXAMPLE 4 · Truss (Whole FBD then Joint FBD)
First draw FBD of entire truss, then FBD of a single joint.
▼ expand

Problem: Simple triangular truss: Pin A at (0,0), roller B at (4,0), apex C at (2,3). Load \(P = 60\) kN↓ at C. Draw: (a) FBD of entire truss, (b) FBD of joint C.

(a) WHOLE TRUSS FBD

Treat the entire truss as one rigid body. Replace pin at A with \(H_A\) and \(V_A\). Replace roller at B with \(V_B\). Apply 60 kN↓ at C. No internal member forces appear.

\(\sum F_x = 0\)
\[ H_A = 0 \]
\(\sum M_A = 0\)
\[ V_B \times 4 = 60 \times 2 \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_B = 30\text{ kN},\quad V_A = 30\text{ kN} \]
(b) JOINT C FBD

Isolate joint C. The two members AC and BC are cut. Replace each cut member with the force it exerts on joint C (assume tension = force pulling away from joint, drawn outward). Apply external load 60 kN↓.

Member angle: \(\theta = \arctan(3/2) = 56.3°\) from horizontal.

\(\sum F_y = 0\) at joint C
\[ F_{AC}\sin56.3° + F_{BC}\sin56.3° = 60 \quad\Rightarrow\quad F_{AC} = F_{BC} = \frac{60}{2\sin56.3°} = 36.1\text{ kN (C)} \]
Compression vs Tension: If the assumed direction (tension = arrow away from joint) gives a negative answer, the member is in compression. Compression means the member pushes on the joint — arrows point toward the joint on the FBD.
EXAMPLE 5 · Portal Frame
L-shaped frame with horizontal load. Internal hinge — requires two FBDs.
▼ expand

Problem: Rectangular portal frame: fixed at A (bottom-left), pin at D (bottom-right). Horizontal wind load \(H = 30\) kN at B (top-left corner) at height 4 m. Beam BD is 6 m wide. Find all support reactions.

WHOLE FRAME FBD

Fixed at A: \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\). Pin at D: \(H_D\), \(V_D\). Total unknowns = 5. But we only have 3 equations → statically indeterminate as drawn. For a simple case, treat as pin at A too or use the given answer approach.

For a pin-at-A (2 unknowns) + pin-at-D (2 unknowns) = 4 unknowns, 3 equations → indeterminate by 1. If D is a roller (1 unknown): total = 3 unknowns → determinate. Assume D is a roller (vertical only):

\(\sum F_x = 0\)
\[ H_A + 30 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad H_A = -30\text{ kN (leftward)} \]
\(\sum M_A = 0\) (taking moments about A)
\[ V_D \times 6 = 30 \times 4 = 120 \quad\Rightarrow\quad V_D = 20\text{ kN}\uparrow \]
\(\sum F_y = 0\)
\[ V_A = -V_D = -20\text{ kN (downward, column in compression)} \]
Lesson: Drawing the FBD of the whole frame first gives the support reactions. Then, to find internal forces (AFD/SFD/BMD per member), draw individual member FBDs using Newton's 3rd law at joints.
Section 08
Common Mistakes
WHAT GOES WRONG AND HOW TO FIX IT
Missing a reaction component
Drawing a pin support with only a vertical reaction (forgetting the horizontal). This leaves the FBD incomplete and equilibrium equations will not balance.
Pin → always draw BOTH \(H_A\) and \(V_A\). Check: 2 unknowns at every pin.
Leaving the support symbol on the FBD
Drawing the triangle/pin/wall symbol instead of replacing it with force arrows. The FBD should show forces only — no support symbols.
Remove every support symbol. Replace with correctly directed reaction arrows labelled algebraically.
Wrong resultant position for UDL
Applying the UDL resultant at the end of the loaded span instead of at its midpoint. This gives the wrong moment arm and wrong reactions.
UDL resultant \(W = wL\) acts at \(L/2\) (the centroid of the load block). Triangular load resultant acts at \(2L/3\) from the zero end.
Not resolving inclined forces
Writing \(\sum F_y = 0\) with the full inclined force magnitude instead of just its vertical component.
Always resolve: \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(F_y = F\sin\theta\). Do this before writing any equilibrium equation.
Taking moments about the wrong point
Taking \(\sum M\) about an arbitrary point that has no unknowns passing through it, creating an equation with 2 or 3 unknowns instead of 1.
Always take moments about a point where unknown forces act. This eliminates them and gives a single-unknown equation directly.
Treating a roller as a pin
Adding a horizontal reaction at a roller support. A roller only prevents displacement perpendicular to its surface.
Roller on horizontal surface → vertical reaction only. Roller on inclined surface → reaction perpendicular to that surface.
Forgetting the wall moment at a fixed support
Drawing only \(H_A\) and \(V_A\) at a fixed support (treating it like a pin). Missing the moment reaction \(M_A\) means 3 equations, 2 unknowns — over-constrained, results wrong.
Fixed support → 3 unknowns: \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\). Always include the moment reaction arrow (curved).
Sign errors in moment equation
Mixing up clockwise and anticlockwise moments or forgetting that a reaction on the opposite side of the pivot has a different sign to loads.
State your sign convention clearly. Draw a + and − rotation direction on the diagram. Check every term: does this force rotate the body clockwise or anticlockwise about the pivot?
