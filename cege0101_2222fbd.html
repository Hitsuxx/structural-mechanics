Q1 — Trusses 🔴 CRITICAL · 4/4 years
METHOD OF JOINTS · ZERO FORCE MEMBERS · MEMBER FORCE SIGNS
PAST PAPER STYLE — Simple Triangular Truss
🔴 CriticalMethod of JointsZFM
A pin-jointed truss has joints at A (pin, left), B (roller, right), and C (apex). A (0,0), B (4,0), C (2,3) m. A vertical load of 60 kN acts downward at C.

(a) Calculate the support reactions at A and B. [4 marks]
(b) Using the method of joints, find the force in members AC, BC, and AB. State whether each is in tension or compression. [10 marks]
(c) If an additional joint D is introduced at (2,0) with members AD and BD connected but no load applied, identify any zero force members. [3 marks]
a
Support reactions: Take moments about A: R_B × 4 = 60 × 2 → \(R_B = 30\text{ kN}\uparrow\). Vertical equilibrium: R_A_v + 30 = 60 → \(R_{Av} = 30\text{ kN}\uparrow\). Horizontal: No horizontal load, pin at A → \(R_{Ah} = 0\).
b
Joint C (loaded joint — start here): Member angles: AC goes from (2,3) to (0,0) → angle = arctan(3/2) = 56.3°. BC goes from (2,3) to (4,0) → same angle. ΣFy = 0: F_AC·sin56.3° + F_BC·sin56.3° = 60 → F_AC + F_BC = 72.1 kN. ΣFx = 0: −F_AC·cos56.3° + F_BC·cos56.3° = 0 → \(F_{AC} = F_{BC} = 36.1\text{ kN (C)}\) (compression, pushing into joint C). Joint A: ΣFy: F_AC·sin56.3° = 30 → confirmed. ΣFx: F_AB = F_AC·cos56.3° = 36.1 × 0.555 = \(F_{AB} = 20\text{ kN (T)}\).
c
Zero Force Members: At joint D (2,0) — lies on AB. Members AD and BD are collinear (both horizontal). CD connects vertically. By ZFM Rule 2: 3 members at unloaded joint, 2 are collinear (AD and BD) → CD is a zero force member. AD and BD carry the load in AB.
✓ \(R_A =\) 30 kN ↑, \(R_B =\) 30 kN ↑ | F_AC = F_BC = 36.1 kN (C) | F_AB = 20 kN (T) | CD = ZFM
PAST PAPER STYLE — Pratt Truss, Multiple Loads
🔴 CriticalSection MethodMultiple Joints
A Pratt truss of span 6 m, height 2 m has top chord joints at A(0,2), C(3,2), E(6,2) and bottom chord joints B(0,0), D(3,0), F(6,0). B is a pin support, F is a roller. Vertical loads: 40 kN at C, 40 kN at E (top chord).

(a) Find reactions at B and F. [4 marks]
(b) Use method of joints at B to find forces in BA and BD. [5 marks]
(c) Identify all zero-force members by inspection. [3 marks]
a
Reactions: ΣM_B = 0: R_F × 6 = 40×3 + 40×6 = 360 → \(R_F = 60\text{ kN}\uparrow\). ΣFy: R_B + 60 = 80 → \(R_B = 20\text{ kN}\uparrow\). ΣFx = 0 → R_Bh = 0.
b
Joint B (0,0): Members meeting: BA (going to A at 0,2 — vertical), BD (going to D at 3,0 — horizontal). ΣFy = 0: R_B + F_BA = 0 (BA is vertical) → F_BA = −20 kN → 20 kN Compression. ΣFx = 0: F_BD = 0... wait, only BD is horizontal and no other horizontal member → \(F_{BD} = 0\) (ZFM).
c
Zero Force Members: BD = 0 (shown above). Check joint D — members BD(horiz), DA(diag), DC(horiz), DF(diag). BD=0 so joint D effectively has BD absent. Re-examine... By ZFM Rule 2 at unloaded joint D: DA and DF are symmetric diagonals, DC is horizontal → CD is ZFM if symmetry holds. Always verify by joint equations.
✓ \(R_B =\) 20 kN ↑, R_F = 60 kN ↑ | F_BA = 20 kN (C) | F_BD = 0 (ZFM)
EXAMINER TIPS — TRUSSES
  • ⚠Sign convention: Tension = member being pulled apart (draw force away from joint). Compression = member being squashed (draw force toward joint). State T or C in your answer — marks are awarded for this.
  • ✓Start at loaded joints or joints with only 2 unknowns. Never start at a joint with 3+ unknowns.
  • ✓Check ZFMs first — identify them before doing joint equations. It eliminates unknowns and saves time.
  • 📐Always calculate member angles from Δx/Δy. L = √(Δx²+Δy²). sin θ = Δy/L, cos θ = Δx/L.
Q2 — Beams & SFD/BMD 🔴 CRITICAL · 4/4 years
REACTIONS · SHEAR FORCE · BENDING MOMENT · DIAGRAMS
PAST PAPER STYLE — Simply Supported Beam, UDL + Point Load
🔴 CriticalSFD/BMDMixed Loading
A simply supported beam AB spans 8 m. A UDL of 15 kN/m acts over the full span. A concentrated load of 60 kN acts at 3 m from A.

(a) Calculate reactions at A and B. [4 marks]
(b) Derive expressions for shear force and bending moment at a section x from A. [5 marks]
(c) Sketch the SFD and BMD, labelling all key values. [7 marks]
(d) State the location and value of maximum bending moment. [3 marks]
a
Reactions: UDL total = 15×8 = 120 kN at 4 m. ΣM_A = 0: R_B×8 = 120×4 + 60×3 = 660 → R_B = 82.5 kN. ΣFy: R_A = 120 + 60 − 82.5 = R_A = 97.5 kN.
b
For 0 ≤ x ≤ 3 m (before point load):
SF(x) = R_A − 15x = 97.5 − 15x
BM(x) = R_A·x − 15x·(x/2) = 97.5x − 7.5x²
For 3 ≤ x ≤ 8 m (after point load):
SF(x) = 97.5 − 15x − 60 = 37.5 − 15x
BM(x) = 97.5x − 7.5x² − 60(x−3)
c
Key SFD values: At A: SF = +97.5 kN. Just before x=3: SF = 97.5 − 45 = +52.5 kN. Just after x=3: SF = 52.5 − 60 = −7.5 kN. At B: SF = 37.5 − 15×8 = −82.5 kN (checks with R_B ✓).
BMD: BM at A = 0, BM at B = 0. Max BM where SF = 0: 37.5 − 15x = 0 → x = 2.5 m (in region 2).
d
Maximum BM: at x = 3 + (7.5/15) = 3.5 m from A. BM_max = 97.5(3.5) − 7.5(3.5²) − 60(0.5) = 341.25 − 91.875 − 30 = BM_max = 219.4 kNm at x = 3.5 m.
✓ \(R_A =\) 97.5 kN, \(R_B =\) 82.5 kN | \(BM_{\max} =\) 219.4 kNm at 3.5 m from A
PAST PAPER STYLE — Cantilever, Multiple Loads
🔴 CriticalCantileverFixed End Moment
A cantilever beam is fixed at A (left) and free at B (right), span 5 m. A UDL of 10 kN/m acts over the full length. A point load of 25 kN acts downward at B.

(a) Find the support reaction and fixed-end moment at A. [4 marks]
(b) Write expressions for SF and BM at distance x from A. [5 marks]
(c) Sketch the SFD and BMD. State where maximum BM occurs. [4 marks]
a
Reactions at A: ΣFy: V_A = 10×5 + 25 = R_A = 75 kN ↑. ΣM_A = 0 (taking clockwise +ve): M_A = 10×5×2.5 + 25×5 = 125 + 125 = M_A = 250 kNm (hogging, anticlockwise).
b
Working from free end (x from B, rightward = negative):
It's clearest to take x from B leftward. SF(x from B) = −25 − 10x (hogging, negative).
BM(x from B) = −25x − 10x²/2 = BM = −25x − 5x² (hogging).
At x=5 (at A): BM = −125 − 125 = −250 kNm ✓
c
SFD: Linear from 0 at free end B to −75 kN at fixed end A. BMD: Parabolic from 0 at B to −250 kNm at A. Maximum BM = 250 kNm at the fixed end A (hogging).
✓ \(R_A =\) 75 kN, M_A = 250 kNm (hogging) | \(BM_{\max} =\) 250 kNm at fixed support
EXAMINER TIPS — BEAMS
  • ⚠SFD must start and end at zero for SS beams — if yours doesn't, your reactions are wrong. Check before moving on.
  • ⚠BMD shape: UDL → parabola, point load → straight lines. Always label peak value and its position.
  • ✓Max BM = where SF = 0 (or changes sign). Set SF expression = 0 and solve for x.
  • 📐Always draw SFD then BMD in that order. The integral of the SFD equals the BMD area.
Q3 — Cross-Sections & Deflection 🔴 CRITICAL · 4/4 years
SECOND MOMENT OF AREA · NEUTRAL AXIS · BENDING STRESS · MACAULAY'S METHOD
PAST PAPER STYLE — I-Section Properties & Bending Stress
🔴 CriticalI_zzσ = My/I
An I-section consists of: top flange 200×20 mm, web 10×300 mm, bottom flange 150×25 mm. The bottom of the section is the datum.

(a) Locate the centroid (neutral axis) from the bottom. [5 marks]
(b) Calculate I_zz about the neutral axis. [8 marks]
(c) A beam of this section spans 4 m simply supported, carrying a UDL of 20 kN/m. Calculate the maximum bending stress at top and bottom. [5 marks]
a
Centroid location:
A₁ = 200×20 = 4000 mm² (top flange), ȳ₁ = 300+25+20/2 = 335 mm
A₂ = 10×300 = 3000 mm² (web), ȳ₂ = 25+300/2 = 175 mm
A₃ = 150×25 = 3750 mm² (bot flange), ȳ₃ = 25/2 = 12.5 mm
A_total = 10750 mm²
ȳ = (4000×335 + 3000×175 + 3750×12.5) / 10750 = (1,340,000 + 525,000 + 46,875) / 10750 = 177.9 mm
b
I_zz using parallel axis theorem I = I_own + Ay²:
Top flange: I_own = 200×20³/12 = 133,333 mm⁴. d₁ = 335−177.9 = 157.1 mm. A₁d₁² = 4000×157.1² = 98.7×10⁶ mm⁴
Web: I_own = 10×300³/12 = 22.5×10⁶ mm⁴. d₂ = 175−177.9 = −2.9 mm. A₂d₂² ≈ 0.025×10⁶
Bot flange: I_own = 150×25³/12 = 195,313 mm⁴. d₃ = 12.5−177.9 = −165.4 mm. A₃d₃² = 3750×165.4² = 102.6×10⁶ mm⁴
I_zz ≈ (0.133 + 98.7 + 22.5 + 0.025 + 0.195 + 102.6)×10⁶ = 224.2×10⁶ mm⁴
c
Max BM: \\(M_{\max} = wL^2/8\\) = 20×4²/8 = 40 kNm = 40×10⁶ Nmm
y_top = (25+300+20) − 177.9 = 167.1 mm
y_bot = 177.9 mm
σ_top = M·y_top/I = 40×10⁶ × 167.1 / 224.2×10⁶ = 29.8 MPa (compression)
σ_bot = M·y_bot/I = 40×10⁶ × 177.9 / 224.2×10⁶ = 31.7 MPa (tension)
✓ \(\bar{y} =\) 177.9 mm | \(I_{zz} =\) 224.2×10⁶ mm⁴ | \(\sigma_{\text{top}}\) = 29.8 MPa (C) | \(\sigma_{\text{bot}}\) = 31.7 MPa (T)
PAST PAPER STYLE — Deflection by Macaulay's Method
🔴 CriticalMacaulay'sBoundary Conditions
A simply supported beam AB spans L = 6 m. EI = 15 MNm². A point load P = 48 kN acts at x = 2 m from A.

(a) Write the bending moment equation using Macaulay's notation. [3 marks]
(b) Integrate twice to find the deflection equation. [5 marks]
(c) Apply boundary conditions to find constants C₁ and C₂. [4 marks]
(d) Calculate the deflection at midspan (x = 3 m). [3 marks]
a
Reactions: R_A = 48×4/6 = 32 kN ↑, R_B = 48×2/6 = 16 kN ↑.
Macaulay BM: EI·d²y/dx² = R_A·x − P⟨x−2⟩ = 32x − 48⟨x−2⟩
Note: ⟨x−a⟩ = 0 for x < a, = (x−a) for x ≥ a.
b
First integration (slope):
EI·dy/dx = 16x² − 24⟨x−2⟩² + C₁
Second integration (deflection):
EI·y = (16/3)x³ − 8⟨x−2⟩³ + C₁x + C₂
c
Boundary conditions:
At x=0: y=0 → C₂ = 0 (Macaulay term absent).
At x=6: y=0 → 0 = (16/3)(216) − 8(4)³ + 6C₁ = 1152 − 512 + 6C₁
C₁ = −(640/6) = −106.7 kN·m²
d
At x = 3 m:
EI·y = (16/3)(27) − 8(1)³ + (−106.7)(3) = 144 − 8 − 320.1 = −184.1 kNm³
y = −184.1 / (15×10³) = −0.01227 m = −12.3 mm (downward)
✓ \(C_1 =\) −106.7 kNm², \(C_2 =\) 0 | y at x=3m = −12.3 mm ↓
EXAMINER TIPS — CROSS-SECTIONS & DEFLECTION
  • ⚠Parallel axis theorem: \(I = \dfrac{bd^3}{12} + Ad^2\). The 'd' is distance from the component's own centroid to the overall centroid. Never forget the 'Ad²' term.
  • ⚠Macaulay brackets: ⟨x−a⟩ = 0 when x < a. When integrating, integrate the Macaulay term as a unit — ⟨x−a⟩ → ⟨x−a⟩²/2. Never expand before integrating.
  • ✓BCs for SS beam: y=0 at both supports. For cantilever: y=0 and dy/dx=0 at fixed end.
Q4 — Frames & Buckling 🔴 CRITICAL · 4/4 years
AFD · SFD · BMD FOR PORTAL FRAMES · EULER BUCKLING
PAST PAPER STYLE — Portal Frame AFD/SFD/BMD
🔴 CriticalPortal Frame3 Diagrams
A symmetric portal frame has: columns AB and CD each 4 m tall; beam BC 6 m wide. A (pin) and D (pin) are at ground level. A horizontal point load of 30 kN acts at B (top-left of left column).

(a) Find all support reactions. [4 marks]
(b) Calculate axial forces, shear forces, and bending moments at key points. [8 marks]
(c) Sketch the AFD, SFD, and BMD for the entire frame. [6 marks]
a
Reactions (4 unknowns, 3 equilibrium eqs + 1 pin condition at internal hinge if present... here no hinge, so statically determinate with 2 pins → 4 unknowns vs 3 eqs → indeterminate).
Correction: with 2 pins at A and D (4 unknowns: H_A, V_A, H_D, V_D) and 3 global equilibrium equations, this is indeterminate by 1. In CEGE0101 typically a hinge is added at the apex or one support is a roller.
With D as roller (V_D only): H_A = 30 kN (←), V_A from ΣM_A = 0: V_D×6 = 30×4 → V_D = 20 kN ↑. V_A = −20 kN (↓). H_A = −30 kN (→), H_D = 0.
b
Column AB: Axial force = V_A = 20 kN (compression). SF = H_A = 30 kN (constant). BM: zero at A (pin), linear to B: BM_B = 30×4 = 120 kNm.
Beam BC: AF = V_A = 20 kN (tension, horizontal). SF = V_A = 20 kN (constant if no vertical load on beam). BM: linear from 120 kNm at B to 0 at C (assuming roller D provides V_D = 20 kN upward).
Column CD: AF = V_D = 20 kN (compression). SF = 0. BM = 0 throughout (D is a pin, no horizontal at D).
c
Diagrams summary: AFD — compression in columns, tension in beam (or zero depending on loading). SFD — constant SF in columns, zero in beam for pure horizontal load. BMD — linear in columns, linear in beam, peak at loaded corner B. Always draw diagrams ON the frame members, with values labeled.
✓ H_A=30kN, V_A=20kN↓, V_D=20kN↑ | \(BM_{\max} =\) 120 kNm at B | Draw all 3 diagrams on frame
PAST PAPER STYLE — Euler Buckling
🟡 High · 3/4 yearsEffective LengthP_cr Formula
A steel column has E = 200 GPa, I = 800 cm⁴, and length L = 3.5 m.

(a) Calculate P_cr for pin-pin end conditions. [3 marks]
(b) Calculate P_cr if the column is fixed at base, pinned at top. [3 marks]
(c) What is the slenderness ratio in each case, using r = √(I/A) with A = 80 cm²? Discuss which condition is more efficient. [4 marks]
a
Pin-Pin (K=1): L_e = 1.0×3.5 = 3.5 m
\\(P_{cr} = \pi^2 EI / L_e^2\\) = π²×200×10⁹×800×10⁻⁸/3.5²
= π²×1,600,000/12.25 = P_cr = 1,288 kN
b
Fixed-Pinned (K=0.7): L_e = 0.7×3.5 = 2.45 m
P_cr = π²×1,600,000/2.45² = π²×1,600,000/6.0025 = P_cr = 2,630 kN (≈ 2× pin-pin, as expected for fixed-pin)
c
Slenderness ratio λ = L_e/r, where r = √(I/A) = √(800/80) = √10 = 3.16 cm = 0.0316 m
Pin-pin: λ = 3.5/0.0316 = 110.8. Fixed-pin: λ = 2.45/0.0316 = 77.5.
Fixed-pin is more efficient — lower slenderness → higher buckling load for same material.
✓ P_cr(pin-pin) = 1,288 kN | P_cr(fixed-pin) = 2,630 kN | λ = 110.8 vs 77.5

Statics & Materials
⚪ Background

Statics is the study of bodies in static equilibrium — where all forces and moments sum to zero. For any 2D structure: ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM = 0. These three equations are your entire toolkit for finding unknown reactions.

Resolving forces: Any force F at angle θ resolves into horizontal component F·cosθ and vertical component F·sinθ.

Key insight: A simply supported beam (pin + roller) has exactly 3 unknowns. With 3 equilibrium equations, it's statically determinate — solvable by hand.

Stress and Strain (Hooke's Law): When a material is loaded, it develops internal stress σ = F/A and strains by ε = δ/L. For elastic materials: σ = E·ε, where E is Young's modulus. For steel: E = 200 GPa.

L03
Trusses
🔴 CRITICAL — Q1 every exam

A truss is a structure of slender members joined at pin joints. Members carry only axial forces — no bending, no shear. Every member is either in tension (being stretched) or compression (being squashed).

Why pin joints? Because pins cannot transmit moments. This simplifies analysis enormously — you only need ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0 at each joint.

Member geometry
\[ L = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2} \qquad \cos\theta = \frac{\Delta x}{L} \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{\Delta y}{L} \]

Zero Force Members (ZFM): By inspection, some members carry zero force — you can identify these without calculations, saving significant time.

Press a rule button to see the ZFM rules

Method of Joints — how to solve any truss:

Find support reactions first using ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM = 0 for the whole truss.
Identify ZFMs by inspection. Cross these out — they are zero and need no further work.
Choose a joint with at most 2 unknown member forces. Apply ΣFx = 0 and ΣFy = 0 to solve them.
Move to the next joint with ≤ 2 unknowns (using known forces just found). Repeat until done.
State tension or compression — positive = tension (arrows away from joint), negative = compression (arrows toward joint).

L04
Beams & Bending
🔴 CRITICAL — Q2 every exam

When a beam bends, internal bending moments develop. The material above the neutral axis is compressed, the material below is in tension (for a sagging beam). This is described by the flexure formula σ = My/I.

Key insight: bending stress is zero at the neutral axis and maximum at the extreme fibres (top and bottom). This is why I-sections are efficient — they concentrate material away from the N.A.

Flexure Formula (Bending Stress)
\[ \sigma = \frac{My}{I_{zz}} \]
M = bending moment (Nm) | y = distance from neutral axis (m) | I = second moment of area (m⁴)
Interactive — Bending Stress Distribution
Physical intuition: Doubling the depth h of a beam increases I by 8× (I = bh³/12), but only doubles the max y. So σ_max halves. This is why deep beams are far more efficient in bending.
L05
Cross-Section Properties
🔴 CRITICAL — Q3 every exam

To use σ = My/I, you need to calculate I_zz for your cross-section. For a composite section (like an I-beam), you split it into rectangles, find each rectangle's own I, then use the parallel axis theorem to account for the offset from the neutral axis.

Label components: Split the cross-section into simple rectangles (top flange, web, bottom flange).
Find each area Aᵢ = bᵢ × hᵢ and its centroid height yᵢ from the bottom datum.
Find overall centroid: \(\bar{y} = \dfrac{\sum A_i y_i}{\sum A_i}\) — this is the neutral axis position.
Apply parallel axis theorem for each rectangle: Iᵢ = bᵢhᵢ³/12 + Aᵢ·dᵢ² where dᵢ = |yᵢ − ȳ|.
Sum them: I_zz = ΣIᵢ. This is your second moment of area about the neutral axis.

Shear Force & Bending Moment Diagrams
🔴 CRITICAL — Q2 every exam

SFD and BMD are graphical representations of internal forces along a beam. They tell you the most dangerous cross-section (maximum moment = maximum stress) and are required for deflection calculations.

Sign convention: Sagging (∪ shape) = positive BM. Shear convention: left portion, upward forces = positive SF.

Relationship between load, SF, and BM: The slope of the SFD equals the distributed load intensity. The slope of the BMD equals the shear force. Where SF = 0, BM is maximum (or minimum).

Standard results — memorise these
\[ M_{\max}^{\,\text{SS+UDL}} = \frac{wL^2}{8} \qquad M_{\max}^{\,\text{SS+P}} = \frac{PL}{4} \]
\[ M_{\max}^{\,\text{cant+UDL}} = \frac{wL^2}{2} \qquad M_{\max}^{\,\text{cant+P}} = PL \quad \text{(at wall)} \]

L07
Beam Deflection — Macaulay's Method
🔴 CRITICAL — Q3 every exam

The governing equation for beam deflection is EI·d²y/dx² = M(x). Integrating once gives slope (rotation), integrating again gives deflection. Constants of integration are found from boundary conditions.

Why Macaulay's method? When loads don't span the full beam, the BM expression changes form at each load point. Macaulay's bracket notation ⟨x−a⟩ lets you write a single unified expression that automatically "switches on" at x = a.

Macaulay's bracket
\[ \langle x - a \rangle = \begin{cases} 0 & x < a \\ x - a & x \geq a \end{cases} \]
Integrate as a unit: ∫⟨x−a⟩ dx = ⟨x−a⟩²/2 (never expand before integrating!)

Virtual Work — Unit Load Method
🟢 Medium — appeared 2022–2023

The unit load method (a form of virtual work) provides deflection at any point without integrating the full deflection equation. Place a unit virtual load at the point and direction where deflection is wanted, find the virtual BM diagram m(x), then multiply by the real BM diagram M(x).

Unit Load Method
\[ \delta = \int_0^L \frac{m(x)\,M(x)}{EI}\,dx \]
m(x) = virtual BM from unit load | M(x) = real BM from actual loads
Shortcut for linear diagrams: The integral ∫m·M/EI dx = (1/EI) × (area of M diagram) × (value of m at centroid of M). Tabulate diagram multiplication products from standard cases.

L09
Frame Analysis — AFD, SFD, BMD
🔴 CRITICAL — Q4 every exam

A frame is a structure where members can carry bending moments — unlike trusses where joints are pinned. In a rigid-jointed frame, the joints maintain their angle under load. Analysing frames means finding the internal AFD (axial force), SFD (shear), and BMD (moment) for every member.

Determinacy: Check before attempting to solve.

Determinacy Check
\[ \\(r + m = 3j + h\\) \]
r = reactions | m = members | j = joints | h = internal hinges | = : determinate | > : indeterminate

Procedure for portal frames:

Find global reactions using ΣFx = 0, ΣFy = 0, ΣM = 0 for the whole frame.
Free body diagram of each member: Cut each member from its connections and show the forces acting on it. The forces at a cut are equal and opposite on the two sides.
Draw AFD (axial forces). For columns carrying vertical load: compression. For horizontal beam carrying thrust: tension or compression.
Draw SFD for each member (shear force). Columns with horizontal point loads have constant SF. Beams with vertical UDL have linearly varying SF.
Draw BMD for each member. Integrate the SFD. BM = 0 at pins (free to rotate). Linear where SF is constant, parabolic under UDL. Draw on the tension side.
WORKED EXAMPLE — Symmetric portal frame, horizontal load H = 20 kN at B ▼ expand
Setup: Columns AB and CD height 3 m. Beam BC width 5 m. A = pin, D = roller. H = 20 kN → at B.
Reactions: ΣFx: H_A = −20 kN (leftward, i.e. →). ΣM_A: V_D×5 = 20×3 = 60 → V_D = 12 kN ↑. ΣFy: V_A = −12 kN (↓).
Column AB: AF = V_A = 12 kN compression. SF = H_A = 20 kN (constant). BM: 0 at A → 20×3 = 60 kNm at B (linear).
Beam BC: AF = V_D = 12 kN (tension, pulls outward). SF = V_A = 12 kN (constant). BM: 60 kNm at B → 0 at C (linear, since no vertical load on beam).
Column CD: AF = V_D = 12 kN compression. SF = 0. BM = 0 (roller provides no horizontal reaction, BM = 0 throughout column CD).

L10
Euler Column Buckling
🟡 HIGH — appears in most Q4s

When a slender column is axially compressed, it can suddenly buckle sideways at a critical load — the Euler critical load P_cr. This is not a material failure; it's a geometric/stability failure. The column snaps into a half-sine wave shape.

Effective length \\(L_e = KL\\) captures the influence of end conditions. A pin-pin column deflects into a half sine wave over its full length (K=1). Fix both ends and the inflection points are at L/4 and 3L/4, so the effective length is L/2 (K=0.5) — meaning it resists 4× the load.

Euler Buckling Load
\[ P_{cr} = \frac{\pi^2 EI}{L_e^2} \]
\\(L_e = KL\\)   |   Pin-Pin: K=1.0   |   Fixed-Fixed: K=0.5   |   Fixed-Free: K=2.0   |   Fixed-Pin: K=0.7

Section 01
What Is a Free Body Diagram?
DEFINITION · PURPOSE · WHY IT MATTERS

A Free Body Diagram is a sketch of a body (or part of a body) that has been isolated from its surroundings, with every force acting on it explicitly shown as an arrow.

Key idea: Remove the object from its environment. Replace every contact point, support, and connection with the force or moment that point exerts on the body. Show ALL forces — including reactions and self-weight.
Why FBDs matter: Without an FBD you cannot write equilibrium equations correctly. Every reaction force, every joint analysis, every moment calculation requires knowing precisely what forces act on the body.

Section 02
The 3 Golden Rules
EVERY FBD MUST FOLLOW THESE
  1. 1
    ISOLATE THE BODY
    Draw the object completely free from all supports, connections, and surrounding structure. Use a clear boundary (a dashed cut-line or just white space) to show it is isolated. The body itself can be a whole beam, a single joint, a section cut, or even a small element.
  2. 2
    REPLACE EVERY SUPPORT WITH ITS REACTIONS
    Each support is removed and replaced with the forces (and moments) it applies to the body. A pin gives two force reactions. A roller gives one. A fixed support gives two forces and a moment. Do not leave any support in the diagram — the whole point is they are replaced.
  3. 3
    SHOW EVERY EXTERNAL FORCE AND MOMENT
    Draw all applied loads (point loads, UDL, moments), self-weight if relevant, and any forces from connections to other bodies. Each force has a magnitude, direction, and point of application. Label everything — unknown reactions get a letter (e.g. \(R_A\), \(H_A\)).
Critical: Internal forces (forces between two parts of the same body) do NOT appear on an FBD of the whole body. They only appear when you cut the body and draw FBDs of each piece separately.

Forces & Load Types
WHAT GOES ON THE FBD
Load Type Explorer
A point load P acts at a single point. It has units of force (kN).
Point Load
Single force \(P\) applied at one point. Units: kN. Drawn as a single arrow at the point of application. Causes both shear and bending.
UDL (Uniform Distributed Load)
Load spread evenly over a length. Intensity \(w\) in kN/m. Resultant \(W = w \times L\) acts at the midpoint of the loaded length for moment calculations.

Intensity varies linearly from 0 to \(w_{\max}\). Resultant \(W = \frac{1}{2}w_{\max}L\) acts at the centroid — \(\frac{2}{3}\) of the length from the zero end.

A couple \(M\) applied at a point. Units: kNm. Drawn as a curved arrow. Contributes directly to moment equilibrium but not force equilibrium. Sign convention: clockwise positive (or state yours clearly).
Inclined Force
Must be resolved into horizontal and vertical components before writing equilibrium equations: \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(F_y = F\sin\theta\).
Self-Weight
Acts downward at the centroid of the body. Often neglected in structural analysis problems unless the problem explicitly says to include it (\(W = mg\) or given as a UDL).

How to Draw an FBD — Step by Step
THE PROCEDURE EVERY TIME
1. Identify
2. Sketch
3. Supports
4. Loads

STEP 1 — IDENTIFY THE BODY

Decide exactly what object you are drawing the FBD for. This could be:

  • The entire beam or frame (to find support reactions)
  • A single joint in a truss (Method of Joints)
  • A section cut through the structure (Method of Sections)
  • An individual member of a frame
The choice determines which forces are internal vs external. A force is external if it crosses the boundary of your chosen body.

STEP 2 — SKETCH THE BODY IN ISOLATION

Draw the body by itself — no supports, no ground, no other members. Keep the geometry accurate (to scale or at least proportional). Draw a light dashed boundary if it helps show what has been removed. The body should "float" in space on your diagram.

Tip: Draw the body shape first, then add forces. Never draw force arrows before the body is sketched.
STEP 3 — ADD SUPPORT REACTIONS

At every point where a support was, draw the reaction force(s) and/or moment that support provides. Use assumed positive directions — if the answer comes out negative, the reaction acts the other way.

Pin at A: Draw \(H_A\) pointing right and \(V_A\) pointing up (both assumed positive).
Roller at B: Draw \(V_B\) pointing up only (no horizontal, no moment).
Don't: Draw the triangular/pin symbol itself. The symbol represents the support in a structural diagram. In the FBD, you only draw the force arrows.
STEP 4 — ADD ALL EXTERNAL LOADS

Draw every applied load: point loads, UDLs (shown as a series of arrows with a line, or replaced by their resultant for moment calculation), applied moments, and self-weight if relevant.

  • UDL of \(w\) kN/m over length \(L\): draw the distributed arrows, note the resultant \(wL\) acts at \(L/2\)
  • Inclined force: draw the force at the given angle, resolve into components for the equilibrium equations
  • Applied moment: curved arrow at the point of application, labelled \(M\) with direction
STEP 5 — LABEL EVERYTHING

Every force arrow needs a label. Known forces get their numerical value. Unknown reactions get algebraic symbols. Dimensions and distances must be marked — you need them for moment calculations.

Reactions: Use systematic notation — \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\) for horizontal, vertical, moment at A.
Distances: Mark the x-position of every load and reaction. Moments need a lever arm.
Angles: If a load is inclined, mark \(\theta\) and the components \(F\cos\theta\), \(F\sin\theta\).
STEP 6 — VERIFY BEFORE SOLVING

Before writing a single equilibrium equation, check:

  • Does every support have its correct number of reactions? (Pin=2, Roller=1, Fixed=3)
  • Are all external loads shown, in the right direction, at the right position?
  • Is the body truly isolated — no support symbols remaining?
  • Count unknowns: for a simply-supported beam, you should have exactly 3 unknowns = 3 equations. ✓
  • Have you marked enough dimensions to write moment equations?
If the FBD is correct, the algebra will work. Most equilibrium errors come from wrong FBDs, not wrong algebra.

Section 06
Equilibrium Equations
WHAT THEY MEAN · HOW TO USE THEM · WORKED EXAMPLE

Static equilibrium means the body is not accelerating — it is either stationary or moving at constant velocity. Newton's second law says \(\sum \mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}\), so if acceleration is zero, the net force must also be zero. In 2D this produces exactly three independent equations.

Equation 1 — No sideways motion
\[ \sum F_x = 0 \]
The horizontal components of every force on the body must cancel out. If they don't, the body would accelerate left or right. Positive = rightward (→) by convention.
Equation 2 — No vertical motion
\[ \sum F_y = 0 \]
The vertical components of every force must cancel out. If they don't, the body would accelerate up or down. Positive = upward (↑) by convention.
Equation 3 — No rotation
\[ \sum M_O = 0 \]
The net turning effect about any chosen point O must be zero. If it isn't, the body would spin. You can choose any O — choosing it cleverly eliminates unknowns.
Why exactly 3 equations? In 2D, a rigid body has 3 degrees of freedom — it can translate in x, translate in y, and rotate. One equation is needed to constrain each degree of freedom. This is why a simply-supported beam (with a pin and a roller giving exactly 3 unknowns) is perfectly solvable: 3 equations, 3 unknowns.
What happens when equilibrium is violated?
All three equations are satisfied. The beam is in static equilibrium.
What Is a Moment?

A moment (or torque) is the turning effect of a force about a point. The further the force is from the pivot, the bigger the turning effect — the same reason a long spanner makes a bolt easier to undo. Mathematically:

Moment of force \(F\) about point \(O\)
\[ M_O = F \times d_{\perp} \]
where \(d_{\perp}\) is the perpendicular distance from O to the line of action of F (the "lever arm")

Sign Convention

Before writing any equation you must decide what "positive" means. This is entirely your choice — but you must be consistent throughout the problem. The standard engineering convention is shown below. Notice that if you assume a direction and the answer comes out negative, that simply means the force acts in the opposite direction to what you assumed. That is not an error — it is the correct answer.

Sign convention — forces and moments
Good habit: At the top of every solution write your sign convention explicitly, e.g. "→ +ve, ↑ +ve, ACW +ve". This prevents errors when you have multiple moments with different directions.
Common error: Changing sign convention halfway through a problem. If you start with anticlockwise = positive for \(\sum M\), every moment in that equation must follow the same rule.
Choosing Where to Take Moments

The moment equation \(\sum M_O = 0\) is valid about any point O in the plane — the fixed end of a beam, a free end, the midspan, even a point off the beam entirely. You always get a valid equation. But the smart choice is to pick O at a point where unknown forces act — because a force applied exactly at O has zero lever arm, so it drops out of the equation automatically, leaving you with fewer unknowns to solve for simultaneously.

Common Mistakes
WHAT GOES WRONG AND HOW TO FIX IT
Missing a reaction component
Drawing a pin support with only a vertical reaction (forgetting the horizontal). This leaves the FBD incomplete and equilibrium equations will not balance.
Pin → always draw BOTH \(H_A\) and \(V_A\). Check: 2 unknowns at every pin.
Leaving the support symbol on the FBD
Drawing the triangle/pin/wall symbol instead of replacing it with force arrows. The FBD should show forces only — no support symbols.
Remove every support symbol. Replace with correctly directed reaction arrows labelled algebraically.
Wrong resultant position for UDL
Applying the UDL resultant at the end of the loaded span instead of at its midpoint. This gives the wrong moment arm and wrong reactions.
UDL resultant \(W = wL\) acts at \(L/2\) (the centroid of the load block). Triangular load resultant acts at \(2L/3\) from the zero end.
Not resolving inclined forces
Writing \(\sum F_y = 0\) with the full inclined force magnitude instead of just its vertical component.
Always resolve: \(F_x = F\cos\theta\), \(F_y = F\sin\theta\). Do this before writing any equilibrium equation.
Taking moments about the wrong point
Taking \(\sum M\) about an arbitrary point that has no unknowns passing through it, creating an equation with 2 or 3 unknowns instead of 1.
Always take moments about a point where unknown forces act. This eliminates them and gives a single-unknown equation directly.
Treating a roller as a pin
Adding a horizontal reaction at a roller support. A roller only prevents displacement perpendicular to its surface.
Roller on horizontal surface → vertical reaction only. Roller on inclined surface → reaction perpendicular to that surface.
Forgetting the wall moment at a fixed support
Drawing only \(H_A\) and \(V_A\) at a fixed support (treating it like a pin). Missing the moment reaction \(M_A\) means 3 equations, 2 unknowns — over-constrained, results wrong.
Fixed support → 3 unknowns: \(H_A\), \(V_A\), \(M_A\). Always include the moment reaction arrow (curved).
Sign errors in moment equation
Mixing up clockwise and anticlockwise moments or forgetting that a reaction on the opposite side of the pivot has a different sign to loads.
State your sign convention clearly. Draw a + and − rotation direction on the diagram. Check every term: does this force rotate the body clockwise or anticlockwise about the pivot?

Section 01
What is a Truss?
DEFINITION · REAL EXAMPLES · WHY TRIANGLES WORK

A truss is a framework of straight members connected at their ends by pin joints, designed so that every member carries only axial force — either tension (being pulled apart) or compression (being squeezed). No member bends. This is the key property that makes trusses efficient: slender members resisting axial loads are far lighter per unit load carried than solid beams resisting bending.

They appear everywhere: railway bridges, roof rafters, tower cranes, bicycle frames — wherever a large span must be crossed with minimum material.

Why triangles? A triangle is the only polygon that is geometrically rigid — you cannot deform it without changing the length of at least one side. A square frame with pin joints at the corners can be racked into a parallelogram. Add a diagonal and you create two triangles — now it cannot deform. The triangle is the structural atom of every truss.
Why not just use a solid beam? A solid I-beam spanning 30 m would be enormously heavy. A truss spanning the same distance uses material only where it is needed — in the top and bottom chords (which carry the main bending effects as axial forces) and in the diagonals (which carry shear). The open lattice structure removes all the material that would otherwise be doing nothing.

Section 02
Key Assumptions
WHAT WE ASSUME WHEN ANALYSING A TRUSS

Real trusses are welded or bolted, members have mass, and loads can be applied anywhere along a member. The idealised truss model makes four simplifying assumptions that make the analysis tractable while giving accurate results for slender structures.

  1. 1
    ALL JOINTS ARE FRICTIONLESS PINS
    Each joint is a smooth pin that can transmit forces but not moments. Members are free to rotate at their ends. Consequence: every member is a two-force member — it can only pull or push along its own axis. No bending moments develop in any member.
  2. 2
    ALL EXTERNAL LOADS ARE APPLIED AT JOINTS
    Loads (point loads, reactions) act only at the pin joints, never along the length of a member. If a distributed load acts on the truss (e.g. self-weight, wind pressure on a bridge deck), it is lumped at the nearest joints before analysis begins.
  3. 3
    MEMBERS ARE STRAIGHT AND WEIGHTLESS
    Each member is a straight line between its two end joints. Self-weight is either neglected (when small compared to applied loads) or distributed as equivalent point loads at the two end joints (half to each).
  4. 4
    CONSEQUENCE: AXIAL FORCE ONLY
    Under these assumptions, every member carries only axial force — either tension \((+)\) or compression \((−)\). The internal force is constant along the full length of the member. This is what makes trusses so easy to analyse: two scalar equilibrium equations per joint, two unknowns per cut.
Real trusses vs the model: In practice, welded connections introduce some bending (called "secondary stresses"), but for slender members (length/depth > 10), the idealised model gives forces accurate to within a few percent. The assumptions are industry-standard and are what you are expected to use in CEGE0101.

Section 04
Determinacy Check
CAN YOU SOLVE THE TRUSS WITH STATICS ALONE?

Before attempting any analysis, you must check whether the truss is statically determinate. The condition comes from counting equations and unknowns. At each joint you can write two equilibrium equations (\(\sum F_x = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\)), giving \(2j\) equations in total for \(j\) joints.

Determinacy condition for a truss
\[ m + r = 2j \]
\(m\) = number of members  ·  \(r\) = number of reaction components  ·  \(j\) = number of joints
\(m + r = 2j\) — Determinate ✓
Exactly enough equations to find all unknowns. Solvable using Method of Joints or Method of Sections. Every member force and every reaction has a unique solution.
\(m + r > 2j\) — Indeterminate ✗
More unknowns than equations. The truss has redundant members — extra members that make it stiffer but require compatibility (deformation) equations to solve. Beyond the scope of CEGE0101.
\(m + r < 2j\) — Mechanism ✗
Fewer members than needed. The structure can move without any member changing length — it is unstable and will collapse under any load. Never seen in a valid design but easy to create by accident.
Interactive — count m, r, j and check determinacy
Exam tip: Always check \(m + r = 2j\) as the very first step of any truss problem, before drawing a single FBD. If it does not balance you cannot proceed with statics alone — state this explicitly in your answer. For a pin + roller support on a 2D truss: \(r = 3\) always.
Section 05
Tension vs Compression
SIGN CONVENTION · HOW TO IDENTIFY · FBD CONVENTION

Every truss member carries one of two types of axial force, and you must state which it is — giving only a number without T or C is incomplete and will lose marks in the exam.

Tension (T) — being pulled apart
The member wants to get longer. Its two ends are being pulled away from each other. The member resists this by pulling back — it exerts an inward force on its joints (pulling the joints toward the member). On a joint FBD, draw the force arrow pointing away from the joint (toward the far end of the member).
Compression (C) — being squashed
The member is being shortened. It resists by pushing outward — it exerts an outward force on its joints (pushing the joints away from the member). On a joint FBD, draw the force arrow pointing toward the joint (away from the far end).
Tension vs compression — forces on joints and member
Tension: the member is being pulled. Force arrows on the joint FBD point AWAY from the joint.
Standard method when solving: At each joint, assume every unknown member is in tension (draw all force arrows pointing away from the joint). Write your equilibrium equations with that assumption. If the answer is positive, the member is indeed in tension. If the answer is negative, the member is actually in compression — simply state the magnitude with (C). Never go back and redraw; the algebra corrects automatically.
Typical pattern under downward loads
Top chord → Compression
Bottom chord → Tension
Diagonals → depends on truss type
Verticals → depends on loading
Physical intuition
Think of a simply-supported beam sagging under load: the top face is compressed (shortening) and the bottom face is stretched (tension). A truss does the same thing but concentrates those effects into discrete members — top chord carries the compression and bottom chord carries the tension.
Section 06
Zero Force Members
IDENTIFY THEM FIRST · SIMPLIFY THE ANALYSIS · 2 RULES

Some members in a truss carry zero internal force under a given loading. Identifying them before you start the main analysis can dramatically reduce the number of unknowns you need to solve for — turning a 9-equation problem into a 5-equation one. There are two rules, both derived from the equilibrium equations at specific joint configurations.

Zero Force Member rules — visual proof
Select a rule to see the visual proof of why those members must carry zero force.
Rule 1 — Two non-collinear members
If a joint has exactly two members meeting at it and no external load is applied there, then both members are zero force members. The proof: write \(\sum F_y = 0\) in the direction perpendicular to one member — it forces the other member's force to be zero; then \(\sum F_x = 0\) shows the first is also zero.
Rule 2 — Three members, two collinear
If a joint has three members, two of which are collinear (form a straight line), and no external load is applied, then the third (non-collinear) member is zero force. The proof: write \(\sum F\) perpendicular to the collinear pair — only the third member contributes, so it must be zero.
Important caveat: Zero force members are zero force only for the specific loading case being analysed. Under a different load pattern (e.g. a moving load, wind, or unsymmetric loading) those same members may become active and carry significant forces. They are never useless — they provide stability, prevent member buckling under load reversal, and carry load during erection.
Exam procedure: Scan every joint for ZFM rules before writing a single equation. Mark each zero force member on your diagram. This simplifies subsequent calculations and shows the examiner you know what you are doing — it usually earns an explicit mark.
Section 07
Method of Joints
FIND ALL MEMBER FORCES · START WHERE YOU HAVE 2 UNKNOWNS

The Method of Joints isolates each pin joint as a free body and applies the two force equilibrium equations \(\sum F_x = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\). Because every member is two-force, the only unknowns at each joint are the member force magnitudes. The strategy is to start at a joint with only two unknown members (which the two equations can solve directly), then move to adjacent joints that now have at most two unknowns remaining.

  1. 1
    FIND SUPPORT REACTIONS FIRST
    Use \(\sum M_A = 0\), \(\sum F_y = 0\), \(\sum F_x = 0\) on the whole truss FBD. This gives all reaction components as known values before you touch any joint.
  2. 2
    IDENTIFY ZFMs AND STARTING JOINT
    Apply both ZFM rules before writing any equations — each zero-force member you identify is one fewer unknown. Then choose the joint with the fewest remaining unknowns (max 2) as your starting point.
  3. 3
    ASSUME TENSION FOR ALL UNKNOWNS
    Assume all unknown members are in tension: draw every unknown force arrow pointing away from the joint. Positive result \(= (T)\); negative result \(= (C)\). No need to redraw.
  4. 4
    WRITE ΣFₓ = 0 AND ΣFᵧ = 0 AT EACH JOINT
    Write \(\sum F_x = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\), resolving every force into components. Solve the two simultaneous equations. State each result with T or C — a bare number loses marks.
  5. 5
    MOVE TO THE NEXT JOINT AND REPEAT
    Each solved member force feeds into adjacent joints. Work joint by joint until all members are found. Check the last joint: if both equilibrium equations are satisfied automatically, the solution is correct.

Section 08
Method of Sections
FIND ONE SPECIFIC MEMBER WITHOUT SOLVING EVERYTHING

The Method of Sections is the right tool when you need the force in just one or a few specific members, particularly in the middle of a large truss. Instead of working joint-by-joint from the end, you make a single cut through the truss, slicing through the target member and up to two others, then treat the portion on either side as a rigid free body with three equilibrium equations.

When to use it: You need the force in a member in the 4th panel of a 10-panel truss. Method of Joints would require solving 6 joints first. Method of Sections jumps straight to it in one cut and three equations.
The golden rule: The cut must pass through exactly 3 members (or fewer if you are lucky with ZFMs). With 3 cut members you have 3 unknowns and exactly 3 equilibrium equations. Choose your moment point to eliminate 2 unknowns at once.
  1. 1
    FIND REACTIONS FOR THE WHOLE TRUSS
    Use the whole-truss FBD to find all support reactions first — you need them to write equilibrium on the cut portion.
  2. 2
    MAKE A CUT THROUGH THE TARGET MEMBER
    The cut must slice through the target member and at most 2 others (≤ 3 unknowns total). Pick the simpler half as your free body.
  3. 3
    DRAW THE FBD OF THE SIMPLER PORTION
    Assume tension at every cut face (arrows pointing away from the section). Include all reactions and loads on that portion. The cut member forces are the three unknowns.
  4. 4
    TAKE MOMENTS ABOUT A SMART POINT
    Pick the point where two of the three cut force lines of action intersect — both vanish from the moment equation, leaving one equation with one unknown. This is almost always the fastest route to the target member.
  5. 5
    USE ΣFₓ AND ΣFᵧ FOR THE REMAINING UNKNOWNS
    Use \(\sum F_x = 0\) and \(\sum F_y = 0\) on the same cut portion to find the remaining two cut-member forces.
Full worked example — method of sections on the same truss
Both methods give identical answers — they are the same equilibrium equations applied differently. Use Joints when you need every member; use Sections when you need one or two specific members in the middle of a large truss.

Joints A at (0, 0) and E at (4, 4): \(\Delta x = 4\) m, \(\Delta y = 4\) m, length \(L = \sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2} = 4\sqrt{2} \approx 5.66\) m.

The left–right part of a reaction force

The up–down part of a reaction force
\(V_A\) is the vertical (up–down) component of the reaction at support A. Both pin and roller supports can provide a vertical reaction. A concentrated force applied at a single point

In Euler's buckling formula, \(P_{cr}\) is the critical load at which a column buckles.
\(P = 60\) kN applied downward at joint E. In the FBD of joint E, this appears as a 60 kN arrow pointing downward, and \(\sum F_y\) at E must account for it.

\(w = 15\) kN/m over \(L = 8\) m gives a total load \(W = 15 \times 8 = 120\) kN acting at the midpoint (4 m from either end).

A turning effect, measured in kNm
\(M\) is a moment — a turning or rotating effect caused by a force acting at some distance from a point. Moment = Force × perpendicular distance. In beam analysis, the bending moment at a cross-section tells you how strongly the beam is trying to bend at that point. At a fixed wall support, \(M_A\) is the moment reaction the wall must provide to stop the beam rotating.
\(M_A = 275\) kNm at the wall of a cantilever means the wall is providing a 275 kNm anticlockwise couple to keep the beam from rotating clockwise under its loads.

\(I\)

How "spread out" the cross-section is, in mm⁴
\(I\) (also written \(I_{zz}\)) measures how efficiently the cross-section resists bending. A cross-section with more material far from the neutral axis has a larger \(I\) and bends less. This is why I-beams have wide flanges — pushing material away from the centre dramatically increases \(I\) without adding much weight. \(I\) depends entirely on the shape and dimensions of the cross-section, not the material.
For a rectangle of width \(b\) and depth \(d\): \(I = \frac{bd^3}{12}\). Doubling the depth increases \(I\) by a factor of \(2^3 = 8\).

How far a fibre is from the bending centre line
\(y\) is the vertical distance from the neutral axis (the line through the cross-section that experiences zero stress in bending) to the fibre you are checking. The bending stress formula \(\sigma = My/I\) shows that stress increases linearly with \(y\) — fibres furthest from the neutral axis experience the highest stress. The maximum stress occurs at \(y = y_{\max}\), which is the distance to the extreme fibre (top or bottom face).
For a 200 mm deep beam, the neutral axis is at mid-height, so \(y_{\max} = 100\) mm. If \(M = 50\) kNm and \(I = 4000\) cm\(^4\), then \(\sigma = \frac{50 \times 10^6 \times 100}{4000 \times 10^4} = 125\) N/mm\(^2\).

Section 01

What is Deflection?

THE ELASTIC CURVE · WHY IT MATTERS · LIMITS IN PRACTICE

When a beam carries load it bends — every point on the beam moves from its original straight position to a new curved position. The deflection \(\delta\) at any point is the vertical distance between the original and deformed position. The complete shape of the deformed beam is called the elastic curve.

Deflection matters for two reasons. First, serviceability: a floor beam that sags 50 mm under occupant load will crack finishes, jam doors, and feel unsafe even if it never yields. Codes limit deflection to typically \(L/360\) under live load. Second, correctness: in statically indeterminate structures, you cannot find the reactions at all without using deflection compatibility equations — the reactions and the deformed shape are coupled.

Serviceability limit: BS EN 1993 (Eurocode 3) limits beam deflection under variable (live) load to \(L/360\), where \(L\) is the span. For a 6 m beam, the maximum allowed sag is \(6000/360 = 16.7\) mm. Exceeding this does not mean the beam yields — it means the building feels wrong or finishes crack.
Sign convention: In this course, positive \(y\) is upward and positive \(x\) is to the right. A downward-sagging beam has negative \(y\) values. Deflection \(\delta\) is usually stated as a positive magnitude with direction stated separately (e.g. "12.4 mm downward").
Elastic curve forming under increasing load — drag the slider

The key quantity we want is \(\delta_\text{max}\) — the maximum deflection, which occurs at the point of greatest curvature. For a symmetric beam under a central load, this is at midspan. For an asymmetric load, it shifts toward the heavier side and must be found by calculus.

Section 02

Theory & Derivation

FROM BENDING MOMENT TO CURVATURE TO DEFLECTION

The relationship between bending moment and deflection comes from two pieces of physics joined together. The first is Euler–Bernoulli beam theory, which links the bending moment \(M\) at a cross-section to the curvature \(\kappa\) of the beam at that point. The second is the geometric fact that curvature equals the second derivative of the deflection curve.

1
Bending stress–moment relationship
\[\sigma = \frac{My}{I} \qquad \text{(bending stress at distance } y \text{ from neutral axis)}\]
The bending moment \(M\) creates a linear stress distribution across the cross-section. The stress is compressive on the hogging side and tensile on the sagging side. \(I\) is the second moment of area of the cross-section and \(y\) is the distance from the neutral axis.
2
Strain and curvature
\[\varepsilon = \frac{\sigma}{E} = \frac{My}{EI} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \kappa = \frac{1}{R} = \frac{M}{EI}\]
Hooke's law gives the strain \(\varepsilon = \sigma/E\). Combining with the geometry of a bent beam shows that the curvature \(\kappa = 1/R\) (where \(R\) is the radius of curvature) equals \(M/EI\). The product \(EI\) is the flexural rigidity — stiffer material (\(E\)) or bigger cross-section (\(I\)) means less curvature for the same moment.
3
Small-deflection approximation
\[\kappa = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \approx \frac{1}{R} \qquad \text{(for small slopes } \frac{dy}{dx} \ll 1\text{)}\]
The exact expression for curvature in terms of \(y(x)\) is \(\kappa = y'' / (1+y'^2)^{3/2}\). For structural beams, the slope \(dy/dx\) is very small (typically less than 1°), so \((1+y'^2)^{3/2} \approx 1\) and curvature simplifies to just \(y''\).
★
The governing equation
Euler–Bernoulli beam equation
\[ EI\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = M(x) \]
\(E\) = Young's modulus (material)  ·  \(I\) = second moment of area (section)  ·  \(M(x)\) = bending moment at position \(x\)
This is the equation you integrate twice. The first integration gives the slope \(dy/dx\) (the angle the beam makes with horizontal at each point). The second integration gives the deflection \(y(x)\). Each integration introduces a constant (\(C_1\) and \(C_2\)) that you find from the boundary conditions.
What is \(EI\)? \(E\) is a property of the material (steel = 200 GPa, timber ≈ 10 GPa). \(I\) is a property of the cross-section shape — for a rectangle it is \(bd^3/12\). Together, \(EI\) is the flexural rigidity: how strongly the beam resists bending. Doubling \(E\) (stiffer material) or doubling \(I\) (deeper section) each halve the deflection for the same moment.

Section 03

Boundary Conditions

THE CONSTRAINTS THAT DETERMINE C₁ AND C₂

Integrating \(EI\,y'' = M(x)\) twice always produces two unknown constants \(C_1\) and \(C_2\). The boundary conditions (BCs) are the known values of deflection and slope at specific points — they are the equations you use to solve for those constants. Each support type imposes specific constraints.

Click a support type to see its boundary conditions
Pin / Roller support
A pin or roller prevents vertical movement but allows rotation. Therefore: deflection \(y = 0\) at the support. The slope \(dy/dx\) is unknown (the beam is free to rotate). Each pin/roller gives you one BC.
\[y(0) = 0 \qquad y(L) = 0\]
Fixed (built-in) support
A fixed wall prevents both movement and rotation. Therefore: deflection \(y = 0\) AND slope \(y' = 0\) at the fixed end. A fixed support provides two BCs — which is why a cantilever with no other support is fully determinate for deflection.
\[y(0) = 0 \qquad y'(0) = 0\]
Free end
A free end (no support) has no constraints on deflection or slope. Instead, the physical constraint is that bending moment \(M = 0\) and shear force \(V = 0\) at the free end. These are natural BCs used when the integration approach requires them.
\[M(L) = 0 \qquad V(L) = 0\]
Symmetry condition
For a symmetric beam with a symmetric load, the slope at midspan is zero by symmetry: \(y'(L/2) = 0\). This is a powerful shortcut — you only need to integrate half the beam and use the symmetry BC instead of the second support condition, which often simplifies the algebra significantly.
\[y'\!\left(\tfrac{L}{2}\right) = 0 \quad \text{(symmetric case)}\]
Section 04

Macaulay's Method

ONE EQUATION FOR THE ENTIRE BEAM · STEP-BY-STEP

The difficulty with direct integration is that the bending moment expression \(M(x)\) changes at every point load and reaction — you need a separate equation for each segment, which creates many constants. Macaulay's method solves this by writing a single \(M(x)\) expression for the whole beam using Macaulay brackets \(\langle x - a \rangle\), which are zero when the argument is negative and equal to \((x-a)\) when positive.

Macaulay bracket rule: \(\langle x - a \rangle^n = \begin{cases}0 & x < a \\ (x-a)^n & x \geq a\end{cases}\). When you integrate, the bracket integrates as a unit: \(\int\langle x-a\rangle^n\,dx = \frac{\langle x-a\rangle^{n+1}}{n+1}\). The bracket is never "opened up" — it stays as a bracket until you substitute a specific \(x\) value.
Full worked example — step through Macaulay's method
UDL with Macaulay: A UDL starting at \(x=a\) is written \(w\langle x-a\rangle^1\) in the loading. After two integrations this gives \(\frac{w}{24}\langle x-a\rangle^4\) in the deflection. If the UDL ends at \(x=b\), add a cancelling UDL at \(b\): \(-w\langle x-b\rangle^1\). This is the standard Macaulay trick for a partial UDL.
Exam procedure: (1) Find reactions. (2) Write \(M(x)\) using Macaulay brackets, starting from the left. (3) Integrate once to get slope \(EI\,y'\). (4) Integrate again to get deflection \(EI\,y\). (5) Apply both BCs to find \(C_1\) and \(C_2\). (6) Substitute \(x\) to find \(\delta_\text{max}\) or deflection at a specified point.

Superposition

COMBINE STANDARD CASES TO SOLVE COMPLEX LOADING

The principle of superposition states that for a linear elastic structure, the deflection due to a combination of loads equals the sum of the deflections due to each load applied separately. This turns a complex loading (e.g. UDL plus point load) into a sum of standard cases you already know.

When superposition applies: The structure must be linear elastic (stress below yield, small deflections). It does NOT apply to buckling problems (which are inherently non-linear) or to structures where the geometry changes significantly under load. For virtually all CEGE0101 beams, superposition is valid.
Superposition — two load cases combining into one result
Example: SS beam, UDL + central point load
Given: \(L=8\) m, \(w=10\) kN/m, \(P=40\) kN at midspan, \(EI=30{,}000\) kNm²
\[\delta_\text{UDL} = \frac{5wL^4}{384EI} = \frac{5\times10\times8^4}{384\times30000} = \frac{20480}{11520000} = 1.78\text{ mm}\]
\[\delta_P = \frac{PL^3}{48EI} = \frac{40\times8^3}{48\times30000} = \frac{20480}{1440000} = 14.2\text{ mm}\]
\[\delta_\text{total} = 1.78 + 14.2 = \mathbf{16.0\text{ mm}}\]
Superposition steps
Step 1: Identify which standard cases apply to each load component.
Step 2: For each case, compute \(\delta_\text{max}\) using the formula. Make sure you use the max deflection for each case (they occur at the same \(x\) only if symmetric).
Step 3: Add the deflections. If they are at different \(x\) positions, you must evaluate each at the point of interest rather than just adding the maxima.
